<section class="section-block">
  <div class="container">
    <h2>The Healing Framework</h2>
    <p>
      Chronic disease (inflammation, pain, mood disorders, metabolic disease) arises from six interconnected systems.
      Gardening addresses all six simultaneously—uniquely among interventions.
    </p>

    <div class="grid-2">

      <div class="framework-card">
        <h3>1. Nutrition (Food Supply)</h3>
        <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Processed food, depleted soil, pesticides, long supply chains</p>
        <p><strong>Gardening solution:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Home-grown vegetables have 2–3x higher micronutrient density</li>
          <li>Access to seasonal, real food (no shelf-life chemicals)</li>
          <li>Soil regeneration increases mineral bioavailability</li>
          <li>Eliminates pesticide load on liver + kidneys</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="muted"><strong>Science:</strong> Nutrient density correlates with soil health (USDA 2020)</p>
      </div>

      <div class="framework-card">
        <h3>2. Movement (Daily Activity)</h3>
        <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Sedentary modern life, sitting 9+ hours/day</p>
        <p><strong>Gardening solution:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Digging, planting, weeding = functional resistance training</li>
          <li>Bending, reaching, squatting improve mobility + posture</li>
          <li>3 hours/week gardening = 150 min moderate activity (CDC guidelines)</li>
          <li>Low-impact; scalable for any fitness level</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="muted"><strong>Science:</strong> Gardening is equivalent to gym exercise for strength + flexibility</p>
      </div>

      <div class="framework-card">
        <h3>3. Stress Reduction (Nervous System)</h3>
        <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Chronic sympathetic (fight-or-flight) activation; anxiety disorders</p>
        <p><strong>Gardening solution:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Nature exposure downregulates stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline)</li>
          <li>Repetitive hand movements calms amygdala</li>
          <li>Soil microbiota (Mycobacterium vaccae) triggers serotonin production</li>
          <li>Outcome focus (growth) reframes attention from worry</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="muted"><strong>Science:</strong> Soil bacteria activate vagus nerve (parasympathetic)</p>
      </div>

      <div class="framework-card">
        <h3>4. Sleep Quality</h3>
        <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Blue light, irregular circadian rhythm, high cortisol at night</p>
        <p><strong>Gardening solution:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Morning/afternoon sunlight (outdoor light 2,000–10,000 lux) sets circadian rhythm</li>
          <li>Afternoon gardening work depletes glycogen → deeper sleep</li>
          <li>Physical activity advances sleep onset + improves REM</li>
          <li>Evening cooldown in garden lowers core temperature</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="muted"><strong>Science:</strong> Outdoor light intensity is 100x stronger than indoor light</p>
      </div>

      <div class="framework-card">
        <h3>5. Community & Purpose</h3>
        <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Isolation, loneliness, lack of meaningful activity; depression</p>
        <p><strong>Gardening solution:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Shared community gardens = regular neighbor contact</li>
          <li>Growing for family/neighbors creates accountability + purpose</li>
          <li>Intergenerational knowledge sharing (grandparent-grandchild)</li>
          <li>Harvest celebration rituals build belonging</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="muted"><strong>Science:</strong> Social connection is strongest predictor of longevity</p>
      </div>

      <div class="framework-card">
        <h3>6. Microbiome Health (Gut Flora)</h3>
        <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Antibiotics, processed food, low dietary fiber destroy beneficial bacteria</p>
        <p><strong>Gardening solution:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Fermented vegetables (garden vegetables → kimchi, sauerkraut) restore flora</li>
          <li>High-fiber produce feeds beneficial bacteria</li>
          <li>Soil exposure rebuilds immune tolerance (not sterility)</li>
          <li>Diversity of vegetables = diversity of bacterial strains</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="muted"><strong>Science:</strong> Gut dysbiosis drives inflammation, mood disorders, metabolic disease</p>
      </div>

    </div>

  </div>
</section>

  <div class="container">
    <h2>Natural Occurrence: Why Gardening Works</h2>

    <p>
      Humans evolved as hunter-gardeners. The last 75 years of industrial agriculture is a historical anomaly.
      Gardening reinstates the nervous system's natural state.
    </p>

    <h3>1. Soil Contact (Mycobacterium vaccae)</h3>
    <p>
      A bacterium naturally present in healthy soil triggers the vagus nerve (rest-and-digest system).
      Hands in soil = direct inoculation with mood-enhancing microbes.
      This is not mystical—it's measurable via serotonin precursors and cortisol reduction.
    </p>

    <h3>2. Circadian Alignment</h3>
    <p>
      Outdoor light (2,000–10,000 lux during day) is 100x stronger than indoor light (300–500 lux).
      Morning outdoor work sets the master clock, enabling proper sleep-wake cycling.
      This requires no devices—just bodies + sunlight.
    </p>

    <h3>3. Movement Variation</h3>
    <p>
      Gardening requires dynamic, varied movement: bending, squatting, reaching, digging, pulling, planting.
      Unlike repetitive gym work, gardening engages small stabilizer muscles + postural chains.
      This is how humans moved for millennia.
    </p>

    <h3>4. Food-Nutrient Density</h3>
    <p>
      Home-grown, soil-grown vegetables have higher micronutrient density than store-bought (often picked immature, shipped 1,000+ miles).
      Eaten fresh (within hours of harvest), nutrient loss is minimal.
      This is measurable via ORAC scores (antioxidant capacity) and mineral analysis.
    </p>

    <h3>5. Fermentation Biology</h3>
    <p>
      Fermented vegetables (from garden vegetables) contain live beneficial bacteria + short-chain fatty acids.
      These feed beneficial gut flora, reduce pathogenic species, and restore normal immune tolerance.
      This is not supplementation—it's food preservation + healing simultaneously.
    </p>

    <h3>6. Social Reciprocity</h3>
    <p>
      Growing for others (family, neighbors, community) triggers intrinsic motivation + purpose.
      Humans are wired for cooperation + reciprocal exchange. Gardening naturally creates this structure.
      Market medicine is transactional. Gardening is relational.
    </p>

  </div>
</section>

    <h2>Practical Implementation: Zone-Based Starter Plans</h2>

    <div class="grid-2">

      <div class="plan-card">
        <h3>Zone 3–5 (Cold climates: Midwest, Northeast)</h3>
        <p><strong>Spring/Summer (May–September):</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Cold-hardy greens (spinach, kale, chard)</li>
          <li>Legumes (peas, beans)</li>
          <li>Root vegetables (garlic in fall → harvest spring)</li>
          <li>Perennial herbs in containers: peppermint, chamomile</li>
        </ul>
        <p><strong>Fall/Winter Storage:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Root cellar or cool basement (potatoes, carrots, onions)</li>
          <li>Fermented vegetables (kimchi, sauerkraut)</li>
          <li>Dried herbs (peppermint, chamomile)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="plan-card">
        <h3>Zone 6–7 (Temperate: Mid-Atlantic, Upper South)</h3>
        <p><strong>Spring/Summer/Fall (April–November):</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Cool-season: greens (spring, fall)</li>
          <li>Warm-season: tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans</li>
          <li>Container herbs: ginger, turmeric (move indoors winter)</li>
          <li>Garlic (plant fall, harvest spring)</li>
        </ul>
        <p><strong>Winter Strategy:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Unheated greenhouse or cold frame</li>
          <li>Stored root vegetables</li>
          <li>Preserved + fermented foods</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="plan-card">
        <h3>Zone 8–9 (Warm: South, Lower South)</h3>
        <p><strong>Year-Round Potential:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Fall–Spring: cool-season crops (greens, broccoli, cabbage)</li>
          <li>Spring–Summer: warm-season (tomatoes, peppers, okra, sweet potatoes)</li>
          <li>Year-round: turmeric, ginger, rosemary, garlic, onions</li>
          <li>Perennials: asparagus, artichokes, herbs</li>
        </ul>
        <p><strong>Advantage:</strong> Extended seasons reduce preservation need</p>
      </div>

      <div class="plan-card">
        <h3>Zone 10–11 (Tropical: Florida, Southern California, Hawaii)</h3>
        <p><strong>Year-Round Growing:</strong></p>
        <ul>
          <li>Turmeric, ginger, citrus, mangos, avocados</li>
          <li>Leafy greens (year-round except peak summer)</li>
          <li>Okra, sweet potatoes, peppers</li>
          <li>Herbs: rosemary, basil, tulsi (year-round)</li>
        </ul>
        <p><strong>Challenge:</strong> Summer heat → afternoon shade + consistent water</p>

    <h2>Measurement & Accountability</h2>

    <p>
      Gardening is not a replacement for medical monitoring. It is a parallel, foundational intervention.
      Track alongside standard medical care.
    </p>

    <h3>Objective measures (with clinician oversight)</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Blood pressure:</strong> Home cuff (twice weekly)</li>
      <li><strong>Blood glucose:</strong> Finger stick (morning) if diabetic</li>
      <li><strong>Sleep:</strong> Simple journal (hours + quality)</li>
      <li><strong>Pain:</strong> 0–10 scale (before/after movement)</li>
      <li><strong>Mood:</strong> PHQ-9 depression screening (monthly)</li>
      <li><strong>Labs:</strong> Annual lipids, glucose, CRP (inflammation)</li>
    </ul>

    <h3>Subjective markers</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Energy level (0–10 scale)</li>
      <li>Stress perception</li>
      <li>Medication side effects</li>
      <li>Social connection (days/week in community garden)</li>
      <li>Dietary quality (processed foods eaten/week)</li>
    </ul>
